Description

Cryptography is a good tool to keep your communication secret. One of the main issues of cryptography is how to send an encryption key to another person over an unsecured communication channel. The solution to it is the Diffie-Hellman key exchange protocol.

In this project, we will tell you the story of Alice and Bob. They are fictional characters commonly used as placeholders in conversations on cryptographic systems and protocols. You will act as Bob, a guy who has an urge to communicate with Alice.

The simplest implementation of the protocol is the following:

  1. Alice and Bob agree to use the modulus \(p = 23\) and base \(g = 5\). These numbers are no secret;
  2. Alice chooses the secret integer \(a = 4\), computes \(A\), and sends it to Bob:\[A=g^a\bmod p =5^4\bmod 23=4\]
  3. Bob chooses the secret integer \(b = 3\), computes \(B\), and sends it to Alice:\[B=g^b\bmod p =5^3\bmod 23=10\]
  4. Alice computes: \[S=B^a\bmod p =10^4\bmod 23=18\]
  5. Bob computes\[S=A^b\bmod p =4^3\bmod 23=18\]
  6. Alice and Bob now share a secret number (\(18\)). They can use the shared secret to encrypt messages.

Look at the math formula below. This is the main idea of the Diffie-Hellman protocol:
\[(x^a)^b=(x^b)^a\]

Let's now rewrite it:
\[(g^a)^b=(g^b)^a\]

Now, add the modulus:
\[(g^a\bmod p)^b \bmod p = (g^b\bmod p)^a \bmod p\]

If something is not clear in the Description, you can read the wiki article that explains the protocol.

In this stage, Alice will tell you \(g\) and \(p\). You are going to need them in later stages.

Objectives

In this stage, your program should:

  • Read a string from the input;
  • Get \(p\) and \(g\) from the string;
  • Print OK.
